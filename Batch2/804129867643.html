Find the solution set of \(2^{2x-2} - 130\times2^{x-1} + 256 = 0\).
We can write this equation as a quadratic in \(2^{x-1}\): \[(2^{x-1})^2 - 130\times(2^{x-1}) + 256 = 0 \text.\]
Using the index law \(a^{m\times n} = \left(a^m\right)^n\) we see that \[\begin{align} 2^{2x-2} & = 2^{(x-1)\times2} \\ & = \left(2^{(x-1)}\right)^2 \text. \end{align}\] Hence the equation \[ 2^{2x-2} - 130\times2^{x-1} + 256 = 0 \] can be rewritten as \[(2^{x-1})^2 - 130\times(2^{x-1}) + 256 = 0 \text.\]
We solve this equation for \(2^{x-1}\) to find that \[2^{x-1} = 128 \text{ or } 2^{x-1} = 2\text.\]
We write \(y\) for \(2^{x-1}\) to make things easier to see, the equation is then \[ y^2 - 130y + 256 = 0 \text.\] We solve this by factorising the left-hand side: \[\begin{align} y^2 - 130y + 256 & = y^2 - 2y - 128y + 256 \\ & = y(y - 2) - 128(y - 2) \\ & = (y - 128)(y - 2) \end{align}\] Hence \(y = 128\) or \(y = 2\). Turning the ‘\(y\)’s back into ‘\(2^{x-1}\)’s, we get \[2^{x-1} = 128 \text{ or } 2^{x-1} = 2 \text.\]
We solve these two equations for \(x\) to find the solution set to the original equation: \[\{2, 8\} \text.\]
Case 1: \(2^{x-1} = 128\).

We solve this by taking the logarithm of both sides and using the law \(\log{a^b} = b\log{a}\):

\[\begin{align} 2^{x-1} & = 128 \\ 2^{x-1} & = 2^7 \\ \log {2^{x-1}} & = \log 2^7 \\ (x - 1)\log2 & = 7\log2 \\ x - 1 & = 7 \\ x & = 8 \text. \end{align}\] Case 2: \(2^{x-1} = 2\).

We solve this similarly:

\[\begin{align} 2^{x-1} & = 2 \\ \log {2^{x-1}} & = \log 2 \\ (x - 1)\log2 & = \log2 \\ x - 1 & = 1 \\ x & = 2 \text. \end{align}\] Hence the solution set is \[\{2, 8\} \text.\]
