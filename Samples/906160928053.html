Find the square roots of \(2 + 2\sqrt3i\).
We express \(2 + 2\sqrt3i\) in trigonometric form:

To write a complex number in trigonometric form, we must find its modulus and argument.

We find the modulus of \(2 + 2\sqrt3i\):

The modulus of a complex number \(a + bi\) is defined as \(\left|a + bi\right| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\).

Hence, \[ \begin{align} \left|2 + 2\sqrt3i\right| & = \sqrt{2^2 + \left(2\sqrt3\right)^2} \\ & = \sqrt{4 + 12} \\ & = \sqrt{16} \\ & = 4 \text. \end{align} \]
\[\left|2 + 2\sqrt3i\right| = 4 \]
We find the argument of \(2 + 2\sqrt3i\):

As \(2 + 2\sqrt3i\) lies in the first quadrant,

\[ \begin{align} \arg \left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right) & = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{2\sqrt3}{2}\right) \\ & = \tan^{-1} \sqrt3 \\ & = \frac{\pi}{3} \text. \end{align} \]
\[\arg \left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right) = \frac{\pi}{3} \]

Substituting these values into the formula \[z = |z|\Bigl(\cos(\arg z) + i\sin(\arg z) \Bigr) \text,\] we get: \[ 2 + 2\sqrt3i = 4\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{3}} + i\sin{\frac{\pi}{3}}\right) \text. \]

\[2 + 2\sqrt3i = 4\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{3}} + i\sin{\frac{\pi}{3}}\right) \text.\]
Using the trigonometric form, we apply De Moivre's Theorem:
\[ \begin{align} \left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right)^\frac{1}{2} & = \left(4\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{3}} + i\sin{\frac{\pi}{3}}\right)\right)^\frac{1}{2} \\ & = 4^{\frac{1}{2}}\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{3}} + i\sin{\frac{\pi}{3}}\right)^\frac{1}{2} \\ & = 2\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{3}} + i\sin{\frac{\pi}{3}}\right)^\frac{1}{2} \end{align} \] De Moivre's Theorem for fractional powers states that \[\left(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta\right)^\frac{1}{n} = \cos\left(\frac{2m\pi + \theta}{n}\right) + i\sin\left(\frac{2m\pi + \theta}{n}\right) \] where \(m = 0, 1, \ldots, n - 1\). Hence \[\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{3} + i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\right)^\frac{1}{2} = \cos\left(\frac{2m\pi + \frac{\pi}{3}}{2}\right) + i\sin\left(\frac{2m\pi + \frac{\pi}{3}}{2}\right) \] where \(m = 0, 1\). Therefore, \[\left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right)^\frac{1}{2} = 2\left(\cos{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2m\pi}{2}\right)} + i\sin{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2m\pi}{2}\right)}\right) \] where \(m = 0, 1\).
\[\left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right)^\frac{1}{2} = 2\left(\cos{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2m\pi}{2}\right)} + i\sin{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2m\pi}{2}\right)}\right) \] where \(m = 0, 1\).

We substitute \(0\) and \(1\) for \(m\) in the above formula and simplify:

When \(m = 0\): \[ \begin{align} \left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right)^\frac{1}{2} & = 2\left(\cos{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2\times0\times\pi}{2}\right)} + i\sin{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2\times0\times\pi}{2}\right)}\right)\\ & = 2\left(\cos{\frac{\pi}{6}} + i\sin{\frac{\pi}{6}}\right) \\ & = 2\left(\frac{\sqrt3}{2} + \frac{1}{2}i \right) \\ & = \sqrt3 + i\text{.} \end{align} \] When \(m = 1\): \[ \begin{align} \left(2 + 2\sqrt3i\right)^\frac{1}{2} & = 2\left(\cos{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2\times1\times\pi}{2}\right)} + i\sin{\left(\frac{\frac{\pi}{3} + 2\times1\times\pi}{2}\right)}\right)\\ & = 2\left(\cos{\frac{7\pi}{6}} + i\sin{\frac{7\pi}{6}}\right) \\ & = 2\left(-\frac{\sqrt3}{2} - \frac{1}{2} i\right) \\ & = -\sqrt3 - i\text{.} \end{align} \]
The square roots of \(2 + 2\sqrt3i\) are \(\sqrt{3} + i\) and \(-\sqrt{3} - i\).
